State Wildlife Action Plans Explorer work in progress

An interactive map and AI assistant for comparing how different U.S. states plan for wildlife conservation — starting with California, Nevada, and Missouri. Each state's plan is kept in its own native structure, so you can see just how differently states approach the same mandate.

About this project

A platform from the Boettiger Lab in the Department of Environmental Science, Policy, and Management at UC Berkeley for exploring State Wildlife Action Plans as cloud-native, queryable geospatial data.

Every U.S. state maintains a State Wildlife Action Plan (SWAP) — a conservation blueprint that identifies the species and habitats most in need of attention and the actions to sustain them — as a condition of federal wildlife funding. But the plans are authored independently, and they look nothing alike: different geographies, different species treatments, different vocabulary, even different names. This app brings several of them into one place without flattening those differences. Each state's layers stay distinct and are labeled by state, so a question like "how does California's approach compare to Missouri's?" becomes something you can actually see on the map.

This is a work in progress. States, layers, models, and capabilities are being added and refined.

The plans do not share a common species key or habitat classification, so cross-state comparison is done explicitly — ask the assistant for, say, "California SGCN species" and "Nevada species distributions" and line them up. Missouri intentionally has no species layer to compare; its plan works entirely through habitat and place.

How it works

The map renders open geospatial datasets directly from cloud-native formats (PMTiles vector tiles, Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFFs, and GeoParquet) hosted on the National Research Platform. No tile server is involved — your browser fetches only the bytes it needs to render the current view.

The AI assistant translates plain-English questions into SQL using DuckDB over the same Parquet files, with column schemas and dataset descriptions discovered automatically through a SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog (STAC). This is how it can answer questions about California's SGCN species list, targets, and strategies — planning tables that have no map geometry of their own.

Low-carbon, open models. The assistant is powered by open-weight large language models (GLM, Qwen3, GPT-OSS 120B, Kimi K2, MiniMax) running on the NRP open-llm-proxy, a shared university inference service running on hardware powered substantially by renewable energy. Compared to large commercial inference services, this reduces both the energy and the data-privacy footprint of every conversation. Carbon and energy use are tracked on our companion sustainability dashboard.

The full agent stack — map, chat, MCP tool server, and STAC catalog — is open-source. See the project repository and the geo-agent core library.

Working with the Data Assistant

If you wish the app could do something — just ask the assistant. Things people often reach for a settings menu to do (adjust transparency, export a CSV, filter a layer, run a cross-dataset query) the assistant can already do in plain language. If it can't, it will say so and usually suggest the closest thing it can.

A few of the actions people most often ask for:

  • Compare states: "Show California's SWAP provinces and Nevada's key habitats", "How do California, Nevada, and Missouri each define conservation geographies?"
  • Adjust styling and transparency: "Make Missouri's forest COAs 40% transparent", "Color the California SGCN ranges by taxonomic group."
  • Show, hide, or filter layers: "Turn on Nevada species distributions and hide California", "Show only bird ranges in Nevada."
  • Zoom and pan: "Zoom to Missouri", "Center on the California South Coast province."
  • Query & combine: "How many SGCN species by taxonomic group in California vs Nevada?", "Overlay PAD-US GAP 1–2 lands on California's SGCN ranges."

A few interface tips that aren't visually obvious:

  • The Data Assistant panel is resizable — drag its left edge; the header button minimizes it, and a floating button restores it.
  • On phones and tablets, the panel auto-collapses to overlay mode so the full map stays visible.
  • Hover over any feature for a tooltip of its live attributes — names, classes, IDs — direct from the source data.
  • Every tool call the assistant runs is logged in the per-turn timeline, with timings, so you can see what it queried and what came back.

Data layers & sources

Each state plan is a public release from its wildlife agency, reprocessed into cloud-native formats by the Boettiger Lab. Click "STAC" beside any entry for the full machine-readable metadata — schemas, column descriptions, license, and download URLs. Entries marked SQL are tabular planning data the assistant can query but that are not drawn as map layers.

California — SWAP 2025 (CDFW)

A nested, relational plan: regions, species, and planning tables. Data © California Department of Fish and Wildlife, CC-BY-4.0.

Provinces & Conservation Units 8 + 46
Eight statewide provinces subdivided into 46 conservation units (terrestrial, freshwater, marine, anadromous), plus marine bioregions, NOAA salmonid ESU/DPS units, and the Bay-Delta unit.

Nevada — SWAP 2022 (NDOW)

A flat, species-centric plan: species ranges and habitat classes, no nested regions or planning tables. Data © Nevada Department of Wildlife (NDOW); redistribution license pending confirmation. Source: NDOW ArcGIS Data Hub.

Species Distributions 258 features
Per-species range polygons for Nevada's Species of Greatest Conservation Need, grouped as Bird, Reptile, Mammal, or Aquatic (fish and amphibians), with a seasonal occupancy attribute.
STACSource
Key Habitats 17 classes
Seventeen statewide key-habitat classes — from Alpine and Tundra to Mojave Warm Desert to Salt Desert Shrub — the habitat framework Nevada's plan is organized around.

Missouri — CCS 2022 (MDC)

A habitat/place-based plan with no species layer: Conservation Opportunity Areas by habitat system, plus landscape-scale priority geographies. Data © Missouri Department of Conservation (MDC); redistribution license pending confirmation. Source: the 2022 Missouri CCS (MDC ArcGIS COA services).

Conservation Opportunity Areas (COAs) 7 habitat systems
Priority areas by habitat system: Grassland/Prairie/Savanna, Forest/Woodland, Glade, Cave/Karst, Wetland, and Stream Reach (aquatic line features). Each COA is a named place identified for focused conservation.
